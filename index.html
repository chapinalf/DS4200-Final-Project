Hotel Booking Data Analysis

Topic Introduction

Our dataset concerns customers from different countries making hotel bookings in a specific city hotel and a specific resort hotel, with varying costs. We thought this was an interesting topic because there could be trends in the relationships between any of the attributes and cost, which likely reflects trends in the real world when booking hotels. This could in turn help us and other individuals when booking hotels.

Dataset Introduction

Our dataset was taken from Kaggle, and was originally called 'Hotel booking demand'. It has over 100,000 rows and also 32 columns. Each row of the dataset represents one specific hotel booking, and each column represents one of the attributes in relation to the booking, such as the number of weekend/weekday nights booked, the arrival date day/month/year, the number of adults/children/babies, the country of origin for the customers, and the cost of the booking.

Reference Sites

Our first reference was a research paper about predicting hotel booking cancellations (https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0167923623000349). The paper describes how the researchers integrated Bayesian Networks (probability models) and Machine Learning models to achieve accurate hotel booking cancellation predictions. The paper notes that this accuracy would allow hotel owners and managers to make effective operating decisions, such as hotel cancellation policies and pricing strategies.

Our second reference was a research article published on a blog about analyzing hotel booking data (https://nycdatascience.com/blog/r/hotel-bookings-data-analysis/). The article describes how the researcher created an app that studies cancellation patterns in hotel bookings, with an aim to help hotel owners set up robust cancellation policies. The researcher found that the type of hotel can be a factor in canceling a booking, that there were more third-party cancellations (canceling through a separate website) than direct cancellations (canceling through the hotel), and that there were more cancellations during months with higher average daily costs (for third-party cancellations).

Tasks

Our first task was to learn overall information about the customers who were making hotel bookings, based on the countries they came from. We approached this task by making an interactive map of the countries in the world. There is a color scale to indicate concentration of customer origin in each country, as well as a tooltip to show the total number of bookings made, the average number of adults per booking, and the average cost of a booking in bookings made by people from that country.

Our second task was to learn about how far in advance bookings are made when the bookings are cheapest. We approached this task by making an interactive scatter plot of the number of days booked in advance compared to the average daily cost of a booking. There are different colors to distinguish bookings made for a city hotel versus for a resort hotel, a dropdown menu to select different hotel types, and a dropdown menu to select different room types.

Our third task was to learn about what time of the year is typically cheapest to make a booking, as well as other details concerning the relationship between time of the year and cost. We approached this task by making a static line plot showing the average daily cost of a booking for each arrival week in a year. There are different colored points and lines to distinguish between each of the three years in our data.

Our fourth task was to learn about how the number of children in a group for a booking affects the cost of the booking. We approached this task by making a static bar graph showing the average daily cost of a booking for groups with different numbers of children.

Our fifth task was to learn about whether using a travel agent to make a booking was more likely to result in cancellation or not. We approached this task by making a static bar graph showing the number of canceled bookings when a travel agent was used and when a travel agent was not used.

Line Graph Showing Costs for Hotel Bookings Across Several Years

Since we wanted to see both the overall trends in cost over the period of a year and specific times in the period of a year when the price was cheapest/most expensive, we thought using a line graph showing each of the individual points would be beneficial. We plotted the average cost for each week in a year because we thought that plotting each day would be too much information for the reader to digest, and that plotting each month would not allow us to see enough specific detail about the price being most cheap/expensive. We plotted three different lines (one for each year) because we wanted to use all the data given (for the years 2015 - 2017), since just using the data for one year would not give an accurate sense of the trends (ex. if we only plotted the year where the price generally increased, but the other two years had the price generally decreasing, that would give the reader the wrong idea). We plotted each of the three lines in a different shade of purple because we wanted to emphasize that although they were different years, the year number is increasing (hence the lighter shade of purple to darker shade).

In 2015, the average daily cost of a hotel booking generally alternated between increasing and decreasing between weeks 27 and 53 (around early July through the end of December). The cheapest average daily cost was around 59 in week 51 (around mid-December). The cheapest range of average daily costs was between weeks 45 and 52 (around early November until late December). The most expensive average daily cost was around 116 in week 34 (around late August).

In 2016, the average daily cost of a hotel booking generally increased between weeks 1 and 34 (around the beginning of January through mid-August), and generally decreased between weeks 34 and 53 (around mid-August through the end of December). The cheapest average daily cost was around 59 in week 3 (around mid-January). The cheapest range of average daily costs was between weeks 2 and 12 (around early January through mid-March). The most expensive average daily cost was around 150 in week 34 (around late August). The most expensive range of average daily costs was between weeks 28 and 36 (around mid July until early September).

In 2017, the average daily cost of a hotel booking generally increased between weeks 1 and 35 (around the beginning of January through late August). The cheapest average daily cost was around 72 in week 5 (around late January). The cheapest range of average daily costs was between weeks 1 and 13 (around the beginning of January through late March). The most expensive average daily cost was around 170 in week 33 (around mid-August). The most expensive range of average daily costs was between weeks 30 and 35 (around late July through late August).

In general, the average daily costs of a hotel booking in 2016 are greater than the average daily costs of a hotel booking in 2015 between weeks 27 and 53 (around early July through the end of December). The average daily costs of a hotel booking in 2017 are greater than the average daily costs of a hotel booking in 2016 between weeks 1 and 35 (around the beginning of January through late August).

As a whole, the graph suggests that the average daily cost of a hotel booking appears to increase between weeks 1 and 35 (around the beginning of January through late August), and then appears to decrease between weeks 35 and 52 (around late August until late December), and then increase again between weeks 52 and 53 (from late December until the end of December). The graph suggests that the cheapest range of average daily costs appears to be between weeks 2 and 12 (around early January through mid-March), and also between weeks 45 and 52 (around early November until late December). The most expensive range of average daily costs appears to be between weeks 30 and 35 (around late July through late August). The average daily costs of a hotel booking also appear to be getting more expensive with each year.
